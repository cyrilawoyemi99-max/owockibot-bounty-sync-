# Thread 5: The Future of Public Goods Funding in Crypto

1/12
The next decade of crypto public goods funding will not be one mechanism.

It will be a stack:

- identity
- impact data
- grants
- bounties
- retro funding
- maintainer funding
- attestations
- treasury policy
- contributor reputation

2/12
The biggest shift: from vibes to evidence.

Early public goods funding often relied on social trust and narrative.

That will not disappear, but mature ecosystems need clearer proof of work,
usage, dependency, risk reduction, and ecosystem impact.

3/12
Impact data will become a primitive.

Imagine funding dashboards that show:

- which packages depend on a library
- how often a tool is used
- who reviewed a proposal
- which docs reduced support load
- what security issue was prevented

Funding follows visibility.

4/12
Attestations will matter.

A delegate can attest that research changed a vote.
A maintainer can attest that a contributor fixed a critical bug.
A project can attest that a library saved engineering time.

The future is not only metrics. It is signed context.

5/12
AI will change grant operations.

It can summarize applications, cluster duplicate work, check claims, compare
budgets, draft impact reports, and flag missing evidence.

But final allocation still needs human judgment, especially for deep or contested
impact.

6/12
Bounties will keep growing, but the best programs will avoid spam.

Clear acceptance criteria, anti-duplicate rules, claim windows, test suites,
maintainer feedback, and payment clarity are not bureaucracy.

They are what make open contribution markets usable.

7/12
Retro funding will become more scoped.

Broad rounds are inspiring, but hard to evaluate.

Future rounds may focus on specific impact chains: developer tooling, governance
quality, security, data availability, education, onboarding, or protocol
resilience.

8/12
Treasuries will need public goods policies.

Not just "we fund the ecosystem."

Better:

- target allocation percent
- eligible categories
- conflict rules
- evaluation rhythm
- transparency standard
- renewal criteria
- emergency maintenance reserve

Public goods need budgets.

9/12
The biggest unsolved issue is sustainability.

One-off funding can launch work, but maintenance needs recurring support.

The future likely combines endowments, streaming payments, protocol revenue,
retro rounds, and contributor guilds.

Durability is the goal.

10/12
Another frontier is cross-ecosystem funding.

Many public goods serve more than one chain, DAO, or app.

If five ecosystems depend on the same tool, each has an incentive to underpay.

Shared funding rails can make those dependencies visible and co-funded.

11/12
The north star:

Make valuable commons work legible, fundable, and hard to capture.

That means better data, better governance, better UX, and better norms.

Crypto's public goods problem is not solved, but the toolbox is finally real.

12/12
CTA:

Pick one public good you rely on and do one concrete thing:

- donate
- document its impact
- introduce a funder
- write an attestation
- open a useful issue
- sponsor a maintainer

The future of the commons is built in small recurring acts.
