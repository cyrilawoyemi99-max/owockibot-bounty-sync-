# Thread 2: Quadratic Funding, Explained Without the Fog

1/12
Quadratic funding is one of crypto's most important public goods experiments.

The core idea is simple:

Small contributions from many people should often matter more than one large
contribution from a single whale.

2/12
In normal matching, $1,000 from one donor can dominate 100 people giving $5.

Quadratic funding flips the signal.

It treats broad community support as valuable information, not just the raw
amount donated.

3/12
That matters because public goods often have many users, each with a small
individual incentive to contribute.

Quadratic funding helps reveal that hidden demand.

If many independent people care enough to donate, the matching pool can amplify
their signal.

4/12
Gitcoin popularized this model in crypto grants.

Projects raise direct donations from the community, then receive extra matching
funds based on the number and distribution of contributors.

The result: funding becomes more participatory, not only committee-driven.

5/12
This changed the culture of grants.

Builders had to explain their impact publicly.
Communities had to decide what they valued.
Funders could support a matching pool instead of picking every winner directly.

That made grants feel more like a market for attention and trust.

6/12
But quadratic funding is not magic.

It has failure modes:

- sybil attacks
- popularity contests
- collusion rings
- shallow marketing over deep maintenance
- short-term campaigns over long-term reliability

Mechanism design always creates new games.

7/12
That is why identity and anti-sybil systems matter.

If one person can pretend to be 100 contributors, the matching pool stops
measuring broad support.

Crypto public goods funding needs both openness and credible resistance to
fake participation.

8/12
Quadratic funding works best when:

- the round has a clear scope
- voters understand the domain
- matching funds are meaningful
- sybil resistance is credible
- impact claims are easy to inspect
- projects can report what changed after funding

Design beats vibes.

9/12
It also works best as part of a portfolio.

Use quadratic funding for community preference discovery.
Use expert grants for deep technical work.
Use retro funding for proven impact.
Use maintainer programs for long-term stewardship.

No single mechanism should do every job.

10/12
The long-term contribution of quadratic funding may be cultural.

It taught crypto communities that users can become allocators.

Funding became something a network does together, not only something a foundation
does behind closed doors.

11/12
The next version needs better measurement.

Donations show preference, but not always impact.

Future rounds can combine donations, usage data, dependency graphs, contribution
records, attestations, and expert review into richer signals.

12/12
CTA:

Before joining the next grants round, ask each project for one sentence:

"What public good gets better if we fund you?"

Then fund the answers that are specific, verifiable, and useful beyond one team.
