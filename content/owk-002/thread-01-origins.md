# Thread 1: Why Crypto Cares About Public Goods

1/12
Crypto did not discover public goods. It inherited an old problem: the most
important infrastructure is often the hardest to monetize directly.

Open-source libraries, wallets, clients, docs, standards, and research make
everyone better off, but rarely send an invoice.

2/12
A public good is useful to many people at once, and one person's use does not
usually stop another person from using it.

In crypto, examples include Ethereum clients, security research, developer
tooling, education, governance research, and community coordination.

3/12
The mismatch is simple:

- everyone benefits from the commons
- only a few people maintain it
- direct revenue is often weak
- the downside of neglect appears slowly, then all at once

That is why public goods funding became a core crypto design challenge.

4/12
Early crypto funding often looked like normal grants: a foundation or ecosystem
team reviewed applications and sent money to promising projects.

This helped many builders, but it had familiar problems: opaque decisions,
slow cycles, and limited feedback from real users.

5/12
The next wave asked a sharper question:

Can blockchains fund shared infrastructure using the same properties that make
blockchains useful?

Transparent ledgers, programmable treasuries, identity primitives, voting,
attestations, and open data all changed the design space.

6/12
This is why crypto public goods funding is more than charity.

It is an ecosystem survival mechanism.

If the shared base layer is underfunded, every app, DAO, rollup, wallet, bridge,
and community eventually pays the tax through fragility.

7/12
Crypto also made coordination cheaper.

Someone can fund a dependency, verify a recipient address, audit a transaction,
and review a public trail without needing the full machinery of a traditional
grant institution.

That does not remove trust. It changes where trust lives.

8/12
The major experiments now include:

- grants
- bounties
- quadratic funding
- retroactive funding
- protocol-level treasuries
- impact attestations
- maintainer guilds
- matching pools
- onchain allocation games

Each solves a different part of the problem.

9/12
The best programs are not just "give money to good people."

They answer three questions:

1. What work matters?
2. Who is credible to judge it?
3. How do we reduce capture, spam, and popularity contests?

Funding is a mechanism design problem.

10/12
The hard part is not sending money. It is deciding what counts as impact.

A great docs page, a bug fix, a governance forum post, a dependency upgrade, or
a security review may be invisible until it saves thousands of hours.

Crypto needs better ways to see that work.

11/12
The history of crypto public goods funding is a shift from patronage toward
systems:

from "who should we help?" to "how do we make valuable work continuously
discoverable, attributable, and fundable?"

That shift is still early.

12/12
CTA:

If your protocol depends on open infrastructure, map the public goods you rely
on this week.

Then ask: who maintains them, how are they funded, and what would break if they
stopped?

That map is where your public goods strategy starts.
