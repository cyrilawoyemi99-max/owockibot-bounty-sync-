## Thread 1 - Public goods are crypto's invisible infrastructure

### Posts

1. Public goods funding in crypto is not charity. It is maintenance for the infrastructure everyone uses but no single app can justify funding alone. 1/12
2. A public good is usually non-rival and hard to exclude: one team using an open library, wallet standard, or research post does not stop others from using it too. 2/12
3. Crypto depends on this layer: clients, bridges, developer tools, audits, docs, grant research, standards, education, and community support. 3/12
4. The market often underpays that layer because benefits are spread across many projects while costs sit with a small maintainer group. 4/12
5. Early crypto copied the open-source default: volunteer energy, foundation grants, donations, and a few corporate sponsors. It worked, but it did not scale cleanly. 5/12
6. The next move was protocol treasuries. If a network captures value, it can allocate a share back to the shared work that makes the network usable. 6/12
7. The hard part is not deciding that public goods matter. The hard part is deciding who gets funded, how much, when, and with what evidence. 7/12
8. Three families of mechanisms emerged: prospective grants, quadratic funding, and retroactive funding. Each solves a different coordination problem. 8/12
9. Prospective grants fund plans before work ships. They are useful for roadmaps, but reviewers must predict impact under uncertainty. 9/12
10. Quadratic funding asks the crowd where matching funds should go. It rewards broad support, not just the largest donor. 10/12
11. Retroactive funding pays after impact is visible. It says: build first, prove usefulness, then let an allocator reward what worked. 11/12
12. CTA: Follow this series for a practical map of crypto public goods funding: QF, Retro Funding, maintainer guilds, and reputation-based bounty rails. 12/12

## Thread 2 - Quadratic funding turns many small signals into matching power

### Posts

1. Quadratic funding starts from a simple idea: public goods should not only follow the largest check. They should also reflect how many people care. 1/12
2. In a normal matching pool, one wealthy donor can dominate outcomes. In QF, many small contributors can unlock a larger match. 2/12
3. The intuition: a project with 200 real backers giving $5 may be more broadly useful than a project with one backer giving $1,000. 3/12
4. That makes QF especially useful for early public goods: docs, dev tools, local education, niche libraries, and community infrastructure. 4/12
5. Gitcoin helped popularize the model in crypto by pairing community donations with sponsor-funded matching pools. 5/12
6. The mechanism is powerful, but not magic. It needs fraud resistance, sybil checks, eligibility rules, and clear round scopes. 6/12
7. If identity is weak, attackers can split one donor into many accounts. If eligibility is vague, rounds drift into popularity contests. 7/12
8. Good QF operators define the round: which ecosystem, which category, which deliverables, which excluded activity, and which review process. 8/12
9. The best signal is not only "who can market hardest." It is broad support from real users who can explain why the work helped. 9/12
10. QF works best when paired with transparency: public applications, open donation data, clear matching rules, and post-round impact updates. 10/12
11. Think of QF as a discovery engine. It surfaces public goods with distributed demand, then gives funders a reason to look closer. 11/12
12. CTA: If your project benefits from shared tools or education, do not only donate. Leave a public note explaining the impact you saw. 12/12

## Thread 3 - Retroactive funding pays for impact after it is visible

### Posts

1. Retroactive public goods funding flips the usual grant question. Instead of "what will you build?", it asks "what impact did this already create?" 1/12
2. The model is attractive in crypto because impact often becomes clearer only after users, protocols, and developers adopt the work. 2/12
3. A library may look small at launch, then quietly become a dependency for dozens of teams. Retro funding can reward that discovered impact. 3/12
4. Optimism made Retro Funding a core part of its public goods strategy: reward contributors whose work supported the Collective. 4/12
5. The benefit is accountability. Builders can point to shipped work, usage, integrations, docs, uptime, or measurable downstream value. 5/12
6. The risk is measurement theater. If only easy metrics count, teams may optimize for dashboards instead of durable usefulness. 6/12
7. Good retro rounds combine evidence types: quantitative usage, qualitative testimonials, maintainer review, security value, and ecosystem fit. 7/12
8. Retro funding also changes builder psychology. It says: create value first, keep receipts, and make impact legible. 8/12
9. The "receipts" matter. PRs, release notes, audits, issue links, adoption stories, and public usage data help allocators reason under uncertainty. 9/12
10. Retro funding is strongest when criteria are announced early, even if payment comes later. Builders should know what evidence will matter. 10/12
11. The future may blend retro rewards with continuous attestations: smaller proofs captured as work happens, not one giant report at the end. 11/12
12. CTA: If you maintain public goods, start an impact log now. Future allocators cannot reward work they cannot see. 12/12

## Thread 4 - Maintainer guilds fund the people behind the protocol

### Posts

1. Some public goods are not projects. They are people doing continuous, high-context work that keeps a protocol alive. 1/12
2. Client teams, researchers, security reviewers, documentation stewards, and standards contributors often create value that is hard to price per task. 2/12
3. A bounty can fund a ticket. A grant can fund a roadmap. But core maintenance needs continuity, trust, and retention. 3/12
4. Protocol Guild is one answer for Ethereum: a collective funding mechanism for contributors to core protocol work. 4/12
5. The insight is simple: if the protocol benefits from ongoing expert labor, funding should not depend only on ad hoc grants or heroic volunteering. 5/12
6. Guild-style funding can make invisible labor more visible: reviews, coordination, tests, research, spec work, and incident response. 6/12
7. It also creates a new governance question: who qualifies, how is membership updated, and how does the system avoid capture? 7/12
8. The answer is not pure automation. Public goods funding needs human review, transparent criteria, and credible ways to challenge mistakes. 8/12
9. Done well, maintainer funding is risk management. It reduces burnout and keeps expert knowledge inside the ecosystem. 9/12
10. Done poorly, it becomes a closed club. The best systems publish criteria, update rosters, and leave a path for new contributors. 10/12
11. The lesson for crypto: not every allocation should chase novelty. Some of the highest ROI funding keeps boring infrastructure reliable. 11/12
12. CTA: When evaluating public goods, ask who maintains the work after launch. Sustainability is part of impact. 12/12

## Thread 5 - The next wave is reputation, receipts, and composable bounties

### Posts

1. The next phase of public goods funding will not be one mechanism. It will be a stack: grants, QF, retro rewards, guild funding, and bounty rails. 1/12
2. Each mechanism has a job. Grants fund plans. QF finds broad support. Retro rewards proven impact. Guilds retain experts. Bounties route focused tasks. 2/12
3. The missing layer is reputation that travels across the stack without becoming a popularity scoreboard. 3/12
4. A useful reputation system should answer: what did this contributor ship, who reviewed it, what value did it create, and was payment settled? 4/12
5. That is why receipts matter. A PR link is a work receipt. A review is a quality receipt. A payout transaction is a settlement receipt. 5/12
6. Put those receipts together and funders can make faster decisions without asking every contributor to rebuild their credibility from zero. 6/12
7. Bounty systems can become more than task boards. They can become public ledgers of scoped work, review outcomes, and contributor expertise. 7/12
8. The danger is overfitting to easy metrics. More claims do not always mean better work. Reputation needs context, categories, and dispute paths. 8/12
9. The best version is portable but humble: evidence-rich profiles, category-specific expertise, transparent criteria, and room for human judgment. 9/12
10. This is where tools like owockibot can fit: connect bounties, proofs, payouts, and contributor history into a cleaner coordination loop. 10/12
11. Public goods funding started with donations and grants. It is moving toward verifiable contribution markets with public evidence and better allocation tools. 11/12
12. CTA: If you run a grant or bounty program, publish the receipts. Future funders, builders, and users need proof trails, not just announcements. 12/12
